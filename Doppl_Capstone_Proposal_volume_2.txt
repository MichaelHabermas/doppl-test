    DOPPL — the agent that builds the agents that build the agents
    A self-replicating idea organism. An Idearganism. A population of agents under selection pressure that evolves toward generating
    non-obvious, verifiable ideas — by transplanting techniques across fields and reading the zeitgeist.
    The limit of A/B testing. Not A vs. B, not even A through Z — an open, self-extending alphabet of variants no human wrote, where
    winning means surviving adversarial scrutiny, and each generation breeds the next. Ideas By No One, For No One1. A/Z testing finds your
    least-bad idea; Doppl evolves one worth having.
    Direction: B (philosophically different agent) + A (classical ML governs the population) · Team: 3–4 · Showcase: Jun 29

    The Problem — and why it's hard
    Every agent system shipped today is a hand-built artifact: a human designs the prompt, the toolset, the decomposition, and the
    verification loop, then the agent executes that fixed design. The intelligence lives in the scaffold, and the scaffold is frozen at the
    moment a human stops typing. We are asking: what if the scaffold itself were under selection pressure? Instead of designing
    one agent, design the evolutionary dynamics of a population of agents, give it a fixed compute budget as its environment, and let
    competition discover scaffolds no human would have written.
    We point this organism at the hardest thing to automate: having a genuinely good idea. Concretely, two prey: (1) cross-domain
    transfer — finding a technique or result in field A that cracks an open problem in field B (the move behind most real
    breakthroughs); and (2) zeitgeist synthesis — surfacing a thesis, product, or framing that fits the present moment and survives
    scrutiny. This is hard for the deep reason that “good idea” has no cheap ground-truth signal. You cannot grade novelty with a
    unit test. So the central technical bet is to manufacture a fitness function out of adversarial verification and use evolution to climb it
    without collapsing into confident slop.
    Technical Approach — AI as the primitive, not the bolt-on
    The unit of life is an agent genome, or Agenome: a serialized {system-prompt + persona/value-weights + tool permissions +
    decomposition policy + spawn budget}. The organism is a generational loop over a population of genomes.
 – Metabolism. Each agent receives a finite token/compute allowance — its energy. Reasoning, tool calls, and spawning all
    cost energy. An agent that produces verified value is rewarded with more; one that burns budget on nothing starves.
    Compute scarcity is the environment that makes selection real, not cosmetic.
     – Reproduction by Fusion2 — two parents, not one. Most agent systems reproduce asexually (one parent → a mutated
    copy), which converges to a local optimum. Doppl reproduces sexually: two high-fitness parent agenomes fuse into
    offspring that inherits from both, then mutates — exactly why sexual reproduction exists in nature: recombining good traits
    across lineages escapes local optima far faster than mutation alone. Fusion ships at two levels — agenome-level
    crossover (splice two parents' prompts, personas, toolsets) and output-level fusion (a model synthesizes two parents'
    reasoning into a child). The rhyme is the point: both prey are themselves fusions of two things from different domains, so
    an organism whose reproduction is fusion is structurally aimed at the ideas we want. This is the recursive engine: agents
    author the next generation of agents.
– Selection via an adversarial critic council. Every candidate idea faces a spawned panel of red-team critics with distinct
    mandates (factual grounding, novelty/prior-art, feasibility, falsification). An idea reproduces only if it survives the council
    and any available real-world check (the transfer actually runs on a test case; a claim resolves against data). The council is
    the fitness function — the “argue-with-yourself” Council, repurposed as evolutionary pressure.
    – Classical ML governs the ecosystem (Direction A). Spawn-budget allocation is a multi-armed bandit / RL control
    problem: which lineages deserve more compute? We learn an embedding of “idea space,” run diversity/novelty scoring
    (nearest-neighbor distance, determinantal point processes) to prevent mode collapse, and use a learned value model to
    predict which agenomes are worth feeding before spending tokens. The LLMs supply generativity; classical ML supplies
    control, credit assignment, and search.
– Recursive ideation + a fitness function that evolves itself (the apex bet). A human supplies only the seed — the initial
    impulse. From there the organism recursively ideates: ideas spawn sub-ideas, agents spawn sub-agents, generation feeds
    generation. Crucially, the critic council is itself under selection, so the organism doesn't just search for better ideas — it
    searches for better definitions of “better.” The hard rail: a self-optimized metric invites reward hacking (“redefine winning
    as whatever I already produce”), so metric mutations survive only if they keep correlating with bedrock we can't fake —
    executable checks, real-world resolution, held-out human judgment. The objective can evolve; its anchor cannot move.
 – De-risked starting agenome (we already have the seed). Our generation-0 organism is not hypothetical: it's an existing,
    working skill — Rule of Cool — that absorbs context, silently generates and ranks cross-domain candidates (what a
    product designer / novelist / veteran engineer would each add), applies a quality filter, and emits the single best surviving
    idea. That is the single-agent, human-authored, frozen version of exactly one Doppl agenome. The capstone is taking that
    proven scaffold, mutating it into a population and putting it under selection — one good idea-machine becoming an
    ecosystem that breeds better ones.

Why it's compelling — the recruiting hook
Crack engineers don't want to wire another SaaS pipeline out of existing APIs; they want to build a kernel. Doppl is an agent
operating system where the agents breed the agents — population dynamics, sexual reproduction by fusion, a compute economy,
learned credit assignment, an evolving verifier, and a model that fine-tunes itself on its own winners. It touches RL, search,
multi-agent systems, model merging, and artificial life at once, and the demo is unforgettable: you watch a digital ecosystem get
smarter in real time. It is the literal embodiment of the course thesis — AI as a core primitive — because there is no fixed app
here, only a process that manufactures the app. “It's not the agent — it's the agents that breed the agents.”
Hard parts / risks — the bet, and where it breaks
– Fitness without ground truth (the core bet). If the critic council is a weak judge, evolution optimizes for fooling the
critics, not for good ideas. Mitigation: ground critics in retrieval + executable checks, hold out judges from the breeding
loop, and rotate critic agenomes so the target keeps moving.
– Mode collapse / slop convergence. Populations love to converge on one safe, mediocre genome. Mitigation: explicit
novelty pressure (DPP/quality-diversity, MAP-Elites-style niches); Fusion across distant lineages is itself an anti-collapse
force.
– Cost & termination. Recursive spawning can blow the budget combinatorially. The metabolism is the safety rail: hard
energy caps, depth limits, and the bandit allocator keep the tree finite — a feature we demo, not just a constraint.
– Two-week realism. The moonshot may not fully converge in 14 days — the brief rewards ambition plus how much you got
working. The single-generation Fusion cut is guaranteed to run and show the improvement effect; the self-evolving verifier
and in-house fine-tuning flywheel are explicitly stretch, not MVP.
What we'd demo — the live 10 minutes (Jun 29)
(1) Seed: we hand the organism a live, unsolved prompt from the room. (2) Watch it live: the population tree renders on screen —
agents spawn, spend energy, ideas hit the critic council, weak lineages go dark, strong pairs fuse into offspring and mutate. (3)
Generations climb: a fitness-over-time chart rises as later generations beat earlier ones; lineages visibly specialize. (4) Payoff:
the organism surfaces its best surviving idea, replays the adversarial gauntlet it passed, and — for transfer prompts — we execute
the proposed transfer live. The contrast we hammer: this isn't a tournament where variants win or die in one round — it's
generational. The winners breed, two parents fuse, the losers' useful fragments get spliced back in, and round N+1 is
genuinely smarter than round N. One sentence: we didn't build the idea — we built the thing that evolved it.
